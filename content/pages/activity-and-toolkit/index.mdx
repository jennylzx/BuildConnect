## Activity and Toolkit

### Phase 1: Co-creation of Seed Themes (the "data")

#### Pre-workshop Preparation of Themes (Data)

With permission from community members, meeting recordings and transcripts were analysed. These included conversations about archival materials, personal experiences, and reflections on museum visits. Insights from this material were synthesised into an initial set of themes, which formed the starting point for the project data.

The initial themes were then reviewed with We Make Museums members during a two-hour workshop at Glasgow Women's Library. The workshop included two activities:

1. **Theme Discussion and Editing**
   Each participant received handwritten Post-it notes representing themes connected to their interests. Participants discussed, edited, and refined these themes together.

2. **Crafting Representations and Links**
   Participants used crafting materials to make themes, and their links to exhibition objects, visible and tangible.

Phase 1 helped community members reflect on their own and others' topics of interest, as shown through active discussion and collaborative editing of themes. For example, one participant changed the term "neurodiversity" to "neurodivergent" to better reflect their perspective.

### Phase 2: (Co-)Design and preparation of familiar physical materials to represent these themes (the puzzle pieces)

Phase 2 focused on the co-design and preparation of physical materials that could represent the themes developed in Phase 1, forming the basis of the puzzle pieces.

To explore this, we organised a participatory physicalization workshop with community members. Using everyday crafting materials, including wool, beads, plasticine, matches, popsicle sticks, paper, and cardboard, participants experimented with different ways of making themes tangible and visible.

### Phase 3: Hands-on assemblage: connecting/modifying/creating puzzle pieces for situated reflection and discussion.

#### Design Objectives

1. To design puzzle pieces in ways that allow each piece to represent one theme in written form.
2. To embed the hierarchy of low-level and overarching themes, and
3. To allow images of exhibition objects to be connected to themes.

#### Final Puzzle Piece Design

The final system comprises three distinct sizes designed for infinite expansion:

- **Small Pieces:** Represent low-level themes.
- **Mid-size Pieces:** Represent overarching themes.
- **Large Pieces:** Represent physical objects selected by community members for the exhibition.

#### Fabrication Process

- **Method:** Laser cutting.

<img
  src={withPrefix("/laser-cutting.jpg")}
  alt="Laser cutting in progress"
  style={{ width: "100%", maxWidth: "800px" }}
/>

#### Material Selection

The process involved rigorous testing of cardboard and mountboard. To ensure durability, the mountboard was sealed using a custom PVA-to-water ratio.

**Note:** The optimal mixture found for sealing the mount board during testing was 100ml PVA to (200ml, 250ml, 350ml, and 400ml) of Water. This is to prevent the mountboard from curling too much. And the best mixing for gluing on the prints onto the mountboard is around 100ml PVA to (300ml - 400ml) of water to avoid it being too easy to peel off.

#### Fabrication Notes

- **Safety:** Laser cutting requires materials that do not produce toxic fumes; water-based PVA is the recommended adhesive for this process.
- **Scaling for Shrinkage:** The top paper layer may shrink after being mounted using PVA. To compensate, scale the print on the Y-axis by 100.01% before mounting, or calculate the specific shrinkage rate (can start with scale the Y-aixs by 99.09%) and adjust the illustrator file prior to cutting.
